# World Lore — Hollowcrown

The world is **Aethermoor**. Names are placeholders until canon is locked, but the events below are canonical for v0+ story content.

---

## The Sundering

Roughly **two hundred years before the game opens**, the world broke. The break was not seismic — it was *ontological*. A thing that had been one became four.

Before the Sundering, a king ruled from a throne we now call only *the Throne Beneath*. He was not a tyrant in the usual sense; he was something worse. He had stopped dying. The kingdom realized this only after his second century. By then it was too late to ask him to stop.

The order of mages who served him — the **Sealing Order** — could not kill him. The arts they had at the time would have unmade the world entire, and the king had grown into the world. So they did the only thing they could think to do.

They **divided him**.

The Sealing Order split the king's existence into four bound parts:

1. **The Crown** — his **body**. The thing that walked, fought, sat the throne. Bound under the Throne Beneath.
2. **The Pact** — his **oaths**. The promises he had given and the ones he had broken. Bound at the bottom of the Drowned Sanctum, kept under salt water by a jailer the Order made for the purpose.
3. **The Memory** — what he had been **before the throne**. The man, the name, the childhood. Hidden in the Voidrift, the gap between real things, where it could not be found and so could not be invoked.
4. **The Will** — what was left. Empty intent. The seat of choosing. Without the body, the oaths, or the memory, the Will was still — almost — *him*. They left it on the throne. Crownless. Waiting.

The Order called this not a prison but **a shape**. Each part held the others. None could move alone. The world did not know what to do with the king afterward, and so it forgot how to talk about him. The Sundering was named for the splitting, not the cause.

The Order who did the splitting died over the next forty years, almost all of them quietly. Their notes were burned by their own hands, except for one.

## The cost

The Sundering did not heal the world. It cracked it. Magic now leaks from the seams the splitting left. Monsters arise from those seams. The kingdoms that survived were small — too small to keep standing armies. They formed the **Adventurer's Guild** to handle what their soldiers couldn't, and they have leaned on it ever since.

Every dungeon in the world is — knowingly or not — built on or near a seam. The deeper one goes, the closer the broken work of the Order. The hollow walls Echo Stones reveal are not coincidence; they are *seals*. Most are still holding. A few are not.

## What is happening now

Each of the four parts of the king has begun to **come back**. None alone is a returning king — each is a fragment that has, over centuries, become a thing of its own. They are dangerous in different ways:

- **The Hollow King** — the body, animate again, holding the empty crown. A fallen guildmaster's body wears it now; the body learns whose it borrows and uses what they knew. Bound under Throne Beneath until his seal failed.
- **The Drowned Warden** — the jailer the Order made to keep the Pact. He outlasted his charges. He has been alone with broken oaths for two centuries. He is no longer entirely sane.
- **The Forgotten** — the void where the king's name was kept. Not a creature; an *erasure*. Killing it does not destroy a thing; it ends a hiding-place.
- **The Crownless One** — the Will. He sits the throne. He has been sitting it for two centuries. He has had time to think.

Killing each of the four sounds like victory. It is, in the small. It is also the **dismantling of the shape**. The Order made the four parts hold each other in place. Each kill loosens what is left.

The player does not know this when they begin.

## The truth — for design reference

The Sealing Order's last surviving notebook is held in the Adventurer's Guild archive. **Brenna's great-aunt Yorath** was one of the mages who did the splitting. The notebook is not a public document. Brenna read it as a girl. She has not opened it in a long time, but she remembers it.

When the player has defeated **two of the four bosses**, Brenna calls the player back to the Guild. She tells them what they have done. The player's choice from there is real:

- **Continue** — undo all four seals. The Crownless One must be faced. What happens after is not a victory in any clean sense.
- **Stop** — refuse to break the last seals. The world holds. Bosses already killed cannot be put back; the Order's shape is broken. But the king does not return. (Future content can build on this branch.)

The player's "save the world" framing was always wrong. The bosses were not threats; they were *containers*. Hollowcrown is, at heart, about discovering that the heroic action was the wrong action — and choosing what to do with that knowledge.

## Tone & style

Lore copy must read **weathered, melancholic, dignified, ambiguous**. No exclamations. No dice notation. No meta references. No winks. Names should sound real (compare: Yorath, Aelia Ren, Roa, Hesta — not Stormblade, Darkwood). Buildings, towns, and monsters carry the same register: *Drowned Sanctum*, not *Cursed Castle of Doom*.

When in doubt: imagine a stone-cutter writing the line on a grave thirty years after the death. That register is the floor.
